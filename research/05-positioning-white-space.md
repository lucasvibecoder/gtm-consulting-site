# Module 5: Positioning White Space

**Date:** February 2026
**Goal:** Identify where Lucas Linares has a defensible positioning advantage based on competitive gaps, buyer needs, and unique background.

---

## 1. OVERUSED ANGLES — What Everyone Says

These positioning themes appear across nearly every competitor in the fractional GTM engineer and Clay agency space. Using any of these as a primary differentiator puts you in a crowded lane where you'll compete on price and reputation — neither of which favors a new entrant.

### The "Build Your Outbound Engine" Angle
- ColdIQ, Growth Engine X, FullFunnel, NewEdge Growth, SalesCaptain, and most Clay agencies all lead with some variation of "we build your outbound engine" or "automated revenue systems."
- This is table stakes. Every agency says it. Buyers have heard it dozens of times and it no longer signals differentiation.

### The "AI-Powered Lead Gen" Angle
- Workflows.io ("AI-first growth studio"), Atherial AI ("custom AI agent developer"), Enablement ("AI-Allbound motion"), GTM Studios, and nearly every Clay agency invoke AI as the differentiator.
- AI is an ingredient, not a positioning. Saying "we use AI" in 2026 is like saying "we use email" in 2010. Buyers assume it.

### The "More Pipeline, Less BDRs" Angle
- Enablement, Redline Growth ("Grow Revenue, Not Headcount"), ColdIQ, and multiple agencies position around headcount reduction.
- True but generic. Everyone making this claim competes for the same buyer who already knows they need fewer BDRs — the question is *how*, not *whether*.

### The "GTM Engineering for B2B" Angle
- The GTM Factory, OneThousandPieces, The Kiln, LeanScale all use variations of "GTM engineering" as a category label.
- The term is becoming commoditized. It describes a role, not a value proposition. Buyers still don't fully understand what it means, so leading with the category label creates confusion rather than clarity.

### The "We're Ex-Clay" Angle
- Eric Nowoslawski (Growth Engine X), Sagar Tiwari (Blue Modern), The Kiln (team of early Clay employees).
- Multiple people claim this. It's a credibility signal, not a positioning. Useful as proof point, dangerous as headline.

### The "Data-Driven Personalization at Scale" Angle
- Growth Engine X (20+ proprietary databases), ColdIQ (multi-channel flywheel), Sculpted (CRM enrichment).
- Everyone claims personalization. The mechanics differ but the promise sounds identical to buyers scanning landing pages.

### The "Done-for-You Execution" Angle
- ColdIQ, Growth Engine X, The Kiln, SalesCaptain — all promise to handle everything while the client focuses on closing.
- Proven model but creates the dependency that buyers increasingly resent (per Module 2 research). Also positions you as a vendor, not a partner.

## 2. UNDERSERVED GAPS — What Buyers Want But Nobody Is Clearly Offering

These gaps emerge from cross-referencing what buyers say frustrates them (Module 2) with what competitors actually deliver (Module 1) and where Clay implementations fail (Module 3).

### Gap 1: The "Clay to Pipeline" Translation Layer
**The problem:** Companies buy Clay expecting pipeline. What they get is a spreadsheet IDE with a credit meter. Module 3 research shows 42% of users burn through credits while learning, 28% cite the learning curve as a major complaint, and 18% report data quality failures. The gap between purchasing Clay and actually generating pipeline is enormous — and nobody is explicitly positioning around closing that specific gap.

**What competitors do instead:** Agencies offer to run Clay *for* clients (creating dependency). Consultants offer to *teach* Clay (assuming clients want to learn). Neither addresses the core buyer need: "I bought this thing, I need pipeline from it, and I don't want to become a Clay expert to get there."

### Gap 2: Signal Architecture (Not Just Signal Monitoring)
**The problem:** Buyers want signal-based GTM but most competitors offer signal *monitoring* — tracking job postings, funding events, technographic changes. Smoke Signals (Nick Zeckets) comes closest to signal *architecture* (designing custom detection systems), but at a price point and engagement length ($50K-$150K, 90-day builds) that excludes most Series A/B companies.

**What's missing:** A practitioner who can *design* what signals matter for a specific business, *build* the detection and scoring logic in Clay, and *connect* signals to automated plays — not just monitor off-the-shelf intent data. The intellectual work of determining *which* signals predict buying readiness for *your* specific product is where the real value lives, and almost nobody positions around it.

### Gap 3: Prompt Engineering as GTM Infrastructure
**The problem:** Module 3 research shows organizations without dedicated prompt engineering guidance waste 30-50% of their AI investment. Every Clay workflow that uses AI (qualification, personalization, research agents) lives or dies on prompt quality. Yet no competitor explicitly positions prompt engineering expertise as a core GTM infrastructure capability.

**What competitors do instead:** Blueprint GTM (Jordan Crawford) teaches prompt engineering within his course ecosystem. Jacob Dietle (Taste Systems) focuses on context management. But nobody says: "Your prompts are your GTM infrastructure now, and most of yours are bad."

### Gap 4: The "Builder Who Sells" Perspective
**The problem:** Buyers complain that agencies lack understanding of their business model (Module 2). GTM engineers build technically proficient systems that produce poor business outcomes because they lack buyer psychology (Module 3). The market bifurcates into *builders who don't understand sales* and *salespeople who can't build*.

**What's missing:** Someone who has actually carried a quota, done discovery calls, closed deals — AND can build the Clay tables, prompts, and workflows. This combination is rare. Most Clay experts came from ops or engineering backgrounds. Most salespeople can't build anything.

### Gap 5: Teaching Without the $20K/Month Price Tag
**The problem:** Jordan Crawford's implementation service is $20K/month with a 3-month minimum. Smoke Signals is $50K-$150K. Blue Modern is $30K-$150K+. The Kiln is $15K-$50K+/month. These prices exclude the exact companies that need the most help — Series A startups with $1-5M ARR that just bought Clay and can't figure out how to make it work.

**What's missing:** A structured engagement ($3K-$8K/month range) that builds real Clay infrastructure, transfers knowledge, and generates pipeline — targeted at companies too early for premium agencies but too sophisticated for DIY courses.

### Gap 6: Transparent, Documented Systems (Not Black Boxes)
**The problem:** Buyers' #1 complaint about agencies is lack of transparency (Module 2). They don't know what's being done, why, or how to maintain it when the engagement ends. The Kiln and Sculpted offer documentation (Loom walkthroughs), but this is positioned as a feature rather than a philosophy.

**What's missing:** A practitioner whose entire model is built around the client owning everything — documented workflows, annotated prompts, decision logic explained. Not "we'll give you docs when we're done" but "you'll understand every piece of what we build because that's the point."

## 4. TOP 3 POSITIONING OPTIONS — Ranked by Differentiation Strength

---

### Option 1: "The Clay-to-Pipeline Architect" (STRONGEST)

**One-liner:**
> "I turn your Clay subscription into a pipeline machine — built by someone who did it for Clay's own enterprise clients."

**Who it appeals to:**
- Series A/B SaaS companies ($1M-$10M ARR) that have Clay but aren't getting results from it
- Founders and GTM leaders who bought Clay based on hype, burned credits trying to learn it, and now need someone to make it actually work
- Companies spending $800-$2,000+/month on Clay + adjacent tools with little to show for it

**Why it's different:**
- *Specificity of problem:* Not "GTM engineering" or "outbound automation" — specifically the gap between buying Clay and getting pipeline. This is a problem buyers can immediately self-identify with.
- *Credibility proof:* "Built systems for Clay's enterprise clients" is a claim nobody else can make at this level. Crawford teaches Clay. Nowoslawski campaigns in Clay. You built the reference implementations inside Clay Labs.
- *Outcome-focused:* Not "I'll build workflows" — "I'll turn your subscription into pipeline." Buyers don't want workflows; they want meetings booked.
- *Addresses the underserved gap:* Module 3 research confirms this is where the market breaks. 42% burn credits learning, 28% cite the learning curve, 18% cite data quality. This positioning directly targets the largest failure mode.

**Risk:** Could be perceived as too narrow (only Clay). Mitigate by framing Clay as the centerpiece of a broader GTM automation stack, not the only tool you work with.

---

### Option 2: "The Signal Architect" (STRONG — More Premium Positioning)

**One-liner:**
> "I design the signals and plays that tell your sales team exactly who to call, when, and why — built on Clay by someone who architected these systems at Clay Labs."

**Who it appeals to:**
- Series A/B companies ready to move beyond spray-and-pray outbound
- GTM leaders who understand intent data conceptually but don't know how to operationalize it into automated plays
- Companies with enough prospect volume that manual signal monitoring doesn't scale

**Why it's different:**
- *Intellectual depth:* Signal *architecture* (designing what to detect and what to do about it) vs. signal *monitoring* (watching for job postings). Nick Zeckets does this at $50K-$150K. You can do it at a fraction of the cost with direct build experience.
- *From experience:* You built signal → play systems at Clay Labs for real enterprise clients. Not theoretical frameworks — actual production systems.
- *Addresses sophistication gap:* Most Clay agencies set up basic enrichment. Signal architecture requires understanding both the buyer journey (your sales background) and the technical infrastructure (your Clay expertise). Few people have both.

**Risk:** "Signal architecture" may be too abstract for early-stage buyers who don't yet understand signal-based GTM. Better for slightly more mature companies. Could pair with Option 1 as a premium tier.

---

### Option 3: "The Builder Who Carried a Bag" (STRONG — Personal Brand Angle)

**One-liner:**
> "I've been the rep drowning in manual research. Now I build the Clay systems that make sure no rep ever has to do that again."

**Who it appeals to:**
- Sales leaders and founders who are frustrated that their technical hires don't understand sales
- Companies that have been burned by agencies or consultants who built technically impressive systems that didn't actually help reps close deals
- Buyers who resonate with authenticity and practitioner credibility over polished consulting branding

**Why it's different:**
- *Personal narrative:* Nobody else in this space has your specific arc — SDR → AE → Clay Labs GTME → independent builder. This is a story, not just a credential. Jordan Crawford was never an AE. Jacob Dietle is an engineer. Eric Nowoslawski is a marketer. You're the only one who went from carrying a quota to building the systems.
- *Buyer empathy:* Module 2 research shows buyers' top complaint is that agencies don't understand their business. Your sales background means you build systems informed by what actually happens in a sales conversation, not what looks good in a demo.
- *Authenticity:* In a market saturated with polished agency branding, the "I've been where you are" angle cuts through. Especially on LinkedIn/Twitter where personal narrative content dramatically outperforms corporate positioning.

**Risk:** More personal brand than business brand. Harder to scale beyond yourself. Works best for LinkedIn/Twitter content and initial client acquisition, but may need to evolve as you grow.

---

## 5. SYNTHESIS: Recommended Approach

**Lead with Option 1** ("Clay-to-Pipeline Architect") as your primary positioning — it's the most specific, most immediately recognizable problem, and hardest to replicate.

**Layer in Option 3** ("Builder Who Carried a Bag") as your content and personal brand angle — this is how you tell your story on LinkedIn and Twitter, and it's what makes you memorable vs. faceless agencies.

**Reserve Option 2** ("Signal Architect") as a premium service tier or upsell — once a client's Clay infrastructure is generating pipeline, designing custom signal-to-play systems becomes the natural next engagement.

### Positioning Stack:
| Layer | Function | Angle |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **Brand narrative** | Why me, why trust me | "The Builder Who Carried a Bag" |
| **Core offer** | What I do, what you get | "Clay-to-Pipeline Architect" |
| **Premium tier** | Advanced engagement | "Signal Architect" |

This stack gives you a personal story that resonates on social (Option 3), a core offer that solves a specific, widespread problem (Option 1), and a premium upsell for clients who want deeper strategic work (Option 2).
